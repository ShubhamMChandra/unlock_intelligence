# Ideas Backlog

Unimplemented suggestions from agent reviews, prioritized by potential impact. Check off items as they ship. Add new ideas at the bottom of the appropriate section with a date.

---

## High Impact -- Do When Ready

These directly affect conversion or credibility. Prioritize before launch or immediately after founding cohort.

- [ ] **Pricing anchor on site** -- Even without exact pricing, add a comparison: "Less than sending one person to a two-day conference." HR buyers need budget context before they'll reach out. Without it, the contact form feels like a trap. _Source: Business Analyst, Round 1._

- [ ] **Lead magnet / curriculum PDF download** -- Email-gated curriculum overview PDF. Captures interested-but-not-ready visitors. The HR buying cycle is 30-90 days; without a lead magnet, visitors who aren't ready to buy today are lost forever. _Source: Growth Agent, Round 3._

- [ ] **Testimonials section** -- After founding cohort, collect 3-5 quotes focused on TEAM outcomes (not individual career gains). Place between Problem and How It Works sections. Format: name, title, company, one sentence. _Source: all 5 agents, Round 1._

- [ ] **Video sample lesson** -- A 10-15 minute clip of Shubham teaching. Proves teaching ability more than any bio paragraph can. Embed on the landing page above the fold or in the How It Works section. _Source: Visionary, Round 2._

## Medium Impact -- Do When You Have Scale

These require assets, case studies, or volume that we don't have yet. Revisit after 2-3 completed cohorts.

- [ ] **`/teams` dedicated page** -- Enterprise-specific landing page with ROI framing, case studies, and a downloadable one-pager for procurement. Build when you have 2-3 case studies to feature. _Source: Business Analyst, Round 1._

- [ ] **Comparison table vs. alternatives** -- Explicit positioning chart: "How This Compares" section against LinkedIn Learning, Coursera, and in-house training. Frame honestly -- don't strawman competitors. _Source: Product Manager, Round 1._

- [ ] **"Between sessions" practice structure** -- If there is a gap between the two half-day sessions, frame it as a feature: "Your team spends one week applying what they learned before the advanced session." _Source: UX Designer, Round 1._

- [ ] **Company logos in credibility bar** -- Replace text-based wordmarks with official company logos when you have permission and proper assets. Logos are processed faster than text. _Source: Design Excellence Auditor, Round 1._

## Rejected Ideas (and Why)

Keep this list. It prevents relitigating decisions.

### Self-service Stripe checkout at pre-launch

**Who suggested it:** Product Manager, Round 1.
**Who killed it:** Product Manager himself, Round 2, after reading the Business Analyst's objections.
**Why rejected:** Engineering time should go to conversion infrastructure (better forms, lead capture, follow-up automation), not payment processing. At pre-launch with zero customers, self-service checkout solves a problem we don't have yet. Build it when individual enrollment volume justifies the integration work.

### Light theme redesign

**Who suggested it:** Visionary, Round 1.
**Who killed it:** Design Excellence Auditor, Round 2.
**Why rejected:** Dark theme has strong precedent in professional education (Reforge, Maven, Brilliant). The issue was not the dark theme itself but developer-aesthetic decoration (code-style fonts, terminal vibes). Consensus: keep dark, strip the developer decoration, add warmth through typography and spacing.

### Separate "Who It's For" section

**Who suggested it:** UX Designer, Round 1.
**Who killed it:** Business Analyst, Round 2.
**Why rejected:** The hero and problem sections already communicate the audience clearly. At pre-launch, every section without substance dilutes the page. A standalone "Who It's For" section adds length without adding information. If someone reads the hero and doesn't self-select, a dedicated section won't change that.
