# McKinsey-Style Editorial Standards for Unlock Intelligence Content

**Date:** 2026-04-03
**Context:** Deep analysis of actual McKinsey and BCG articles (Economic Potential of Generative AI, Superagency in the Workplace, State of AI 2025, BCG AI Radar) to establish editorial standards for our thought leadership. Our current articles read like college essays — thesis statement, supporting evidence, conclusion. They need to read like consulting thought leadership — proprietary observation, anchored proof, organizational implication.

## Why This Matters

Our content exists to generate a client conversation. An L&D Director reads an article, forwards it to their VP of People or CFO, and thinks "we should talk to these people." For that to happen, the article needs to:
1. Contain data the reader can cite in an internal memo
2. Frame the problem in organizational terms (not "you have a problem" — "organizations face a pattern")
3. Demonstrate a proprietary vantage point the reader can't get elsewhere

## The Three Structural Problems in Our Current Copy

### 1. We assert. McKinsey proves.
Our copy says "adoption looks the same everywhere: early adopters experimenting alone." That's an assertion. McKinsey would write: "While 92 percent of companies plan to increase AI investments, only 1 percent describe their organizations as mature in deployment." The reader can't argue with that. It's anchored. A VP forwarding this to their CFO needs the numbers to make the internal case. We're currently asking them to forward our vibes.

### 2. We address the reader. McKinsey describes the phenomenon.
"Your team is experimenting in isolation" registers as sales copy. "Teams across organizations default to individual experimentation" registers as research. A McKinsey Partner at a working dinner doesn't say "your company has a problem." They say "we're seeing a pattern across our clients" and let the listener self-identify. Write about the phenomenon. The reader will see themselves without being pointed at.

### 3. Our paragraphs wander. McKinsey paragraphs have jobs.
Our paragraphs make five observations in a row with no hierarchy. A McKinsey paragraph has one job:
- **Sentence 1:** State the finding
- **Sentence 2-3:** Provide evidence (data point, named example, specific observation)
- **Sentence 4:** Name the implication (so what for the organization?)

If a paragraph doesn't advance the argument, cut it.

## The Rewrite Framework

### For every section, answer three questions before writing:
1. **What is the one finding this section proves?** Write it in one sentence. That's your topic sentence.
2. **What's the evidence?** One data point (sourced), one named example, or one specific observation from the UChicago/Digital Realty vantage point. Not all three — pick one.
3. **What's the implication for the reader's organization?** The "so what" that earns the section its place.

### Opening Pattern: Familiar Context, Surprising Escalation, Scope

McKinsey never opens with a statistic or a definition. They open with what everyone already believes, then show why it's incomplete.

**Actual McKinsey (Superagency report):**
> "The long-term potential of AI is great, but the short-term returns are unclear. Over the next three years, 92 percent of companies plan to increase their AI investments. But while nearly all companies are investing in AI, only 1 percent of leaders call their companies 'mature' on the deployment spectrum."

Pattern: Tension statement → supporting data → "while...only" gap → framing question

### The "While X, Only Y" Construction

The single most important pattern in consulting prose. Creates productive tension between aspiration and reality.

**McKinsey:** "While nearly all companies are investing in AI, only 1 percent of leaders call their companies 'mature' on the deployment spectrum."

**BCG:** "While 75% of executives rank AI as a top three strategic priority, only a quarter report meaningful value."

Formula: While [impressive positive stat], only/but/yet [deflating reality stat].

We have zero instances of this pattern. Every article should use it at least once.

### Statistics: Anchor-Context-Implication

Never present a naked number. Every statistic needs:
1. **An anchor** — a comparison to something the reader already understands (previous year, competing metric, another country's GDP, industry benchmark)
2. **Context** — embedded in a sentence that tells the reader what the number means
3. **Implication** — what it means for the organization

**McKinsey:** "$2.6 trillion to $4.4 trillion annually — by comparison, the United Kingdom's entire GDP in 2021 was $3.1 trillion."

**Our current copy:** "82% of enterprises invest in AI training, yet 59% still report a skills gap." This is close but needs the anchor: compared to what? What was it last year? What does "skills gap" mean in operational terms?

### Sentence Rhythm: Short/Long Alternation

McKinsey alternates between short declarative sentences (10-18 words) and longer analytical sentences (25-50 words). Never two of the same length in a row.

**McKinsey:** "AI has permeated our lives incrementally. [6 words] As a result, its progress has been almost imperceptible. [10 words] Clear milestones, such as when AlphaGo defeated a world champion Go player in 2016, were celebrated but then quickly faded from the public's consciousness. [24 words]"

### Section Endings: Forward Look, Not Summary

McKinsey closes sections with a forward-looking imperative or a reframing question. Never a summary of what was just said.

**McKinsey:** "The risk for business leaders is not thinking too big, but rather too small."

**Bad:** "In summary, the skills gap remains a challenge."

### Pronoun Discipline

| Use | Don't use |
|-----|-----------|
| "Organizations," "companies," "leaders" | "You," "your team" (in articles — OK on homepage) |
| "We" for institutional authority ("we observe," "our research") | "I" more than 2x per article |
| "Workers," "professionals," "employees" | "People" (too casual for analysis) |

**Exception:** Homepage marketing copy can use "you/your" — it's a different register. Articles should read like analysis, not sales.

### Heading Style

Headings are **declarative claims**, not questions. The heading states what the section will prove. The first paragraph provides the context that makes the claim necessary — it does NOT restate the heading.

| Good | Bad |
|------|-----|
| "The gap between AI ambition and AI fluency is widening" | "The AI Skills Gap Is Already Costing You" |
| "Structured training outperforms self-paced by an order of magnitude" | "Why This Program Works" |
| "Adoption dies when nobody checks" | "Why AI Adoption Dies in Three Weeks" |

### Vocabulary Register

| Prefer | Over |
|--------|------|
| "Transform," "reshape" | "Disrupt" |
| "Augment" | "Help," "assist" |
| "Accelerate" | "Speed up" |
| "Has the potential to," "could," "is poised to" | "Will," absolute claims |
| "Workforce readiness" | "AI fluency" (too branded) |
| "Capability building" | "Training" (when discussing the concept) |

## Critical Calibration: Our Audience Is Not McKinsey's Audience

McKinsey writes for C-suite strategists who read 80-page reports. Our audience is HR/L&D Directors who scan articles on their phone between meetings. They are people-oriented, not analytical. They need:

- **Pre-digested arguments** — don't make them connect dots, connect the dots for them
- **Lines they can repeat in a meeting** without notes ("86% of companies are spending more, but only 35% see results")
- **Short paragraphs** — 2-3 sentences, not 4-6. One idea per paragraph.
- **Every sentence earnable in one pass** — if a sentence requires re-reading, rewrite it
- **Ammunition for internal buy-in** — they're forwarding this to a VP or CFO who will spend 90 seconds on it

The formula: **McKinsey structure** (anchored stats, claim-evidence-implication, "while X only Y") at **HBR blog reading level**. Take the rigor, lose the density.

### Density Test
Read each paragraph out loud. If you run out of breath, it's too long. If you can't summarize it in one sentence, it's doing too many jobs.

### The Forwarding Test
For every section: would an HR Director copy-paste this into a Slack message to their VP? If the language is too elevated or the paragraph too dense, they won't. Make it quotable.

## Where Our Voice Differs From McKinsey (Intentionally)

McKinsey writes as an institution. We write with a named author's perspective. This is our differentiator — Shubham's dual vantage point (UChicago classroom + Digital Realty enterprise) is something McKinsey can't replicate. So:

- **Use "I" sparingly (2-3x per article max)** for observations only Shubham could make: "I've watched this same pattern play out in a graduate classroom and a Fortune 500 conference room."
- **Name specific products** where McKinsey would say "leading AI platforms": ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, Custom GPTs.
- **Be more direct** in section endings. McKinsey hedges with "could" and "has the potential to." We can afford to be more pointed because we're practitioners, not researchers.
- **Homepage copy stays in "you/your" register.** The articles shift to organizational framing. The homepage speaks to the buyer directly. The articles speak about the phenomenon and let the buyer self-identify.

## Checklist Before Publishing

- [ ] Every claim has a sourced data point or named example
- [ ] Every statistic has a comparison anchor
- [ ] At least one "while X, only Y" construction per article
- [ ] No paragraph longer than 3 sentences (2 is better)
- [ ] Sentence rhythm alternates short/long
- [ ] Section endings are forward-looking, not summaries
- [ ] "You/your" appears zero times in article body (OK in homepage copy)
- [ ] "I" appears no more than 2-3 times, always for proprietary observations
- [ ] Headings are declarative claims
- [ ] First paragraph does not restate the heading
