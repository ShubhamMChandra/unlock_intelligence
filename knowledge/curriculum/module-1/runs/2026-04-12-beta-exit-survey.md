# Exit survey — 2026-04-12 beta (anonymous)

**Purpose:** Catch what attendees won't say to your face. The round-table debrief captures group energy and positive signal; this survey captures the quiet truth.

**Format:** 7 questions, ~5 minutes to complete. Anonymous. Can be handed out as paper at the end of the debrief, or dropped as a Google Form link in the group chat while you're still in the room. Paper is probably better for this first run — the act of writing physically commits people to finishing it before they leave.

**Printing (MD → PDF):** This file renders cleanly through Pandoc (`pandoc input.md -o output.pdf`) or any modern markdown previewer with PDF export (Typora, Obsidian, VS Code's Markdown PDF extension, or the macOS Marked 2 app). The write-in lines use code-span underscores (backtick-wrapped) which are guaranteed to render as monospace underscores in any tool — no risk of being interpreted as italic markers or thematic breaks.

**When to hand it out:** Right after the verbal debrief round-table, before anyone stands up. Say it out loud: *"Last thing. I'm going to pass out a one-page survey. Five minutes. No names. Please fill it out before you leave — if you take it home, you won't."*

**How to collect:** Have a single envelope or folder at the door. Everyone drops theirs in as they leave. Don't read any of them in front of the group.

## Notes for the instructor

**Why these 7 and not others:** Question 1 is NPS because it's the single most compressed willingness-to-recommend signal you can get. Questions 2 and 3 are the asymmetric pair — best moment and worst moment — because paired specificity catches what a generic "how was it" question misses. Question 4 surfaces gaps without making the respondent write a whole critique. Question 5 is the intent-to-use check, which is the real-world test of whether the lesson created any behavioral change or just good vibes. Question 6 is willingness-to-pay, which is the closest thing to a commercial signal you can get at a friends beta. Question 7 is the "brutal" open box — the honest thing they didn't know how to bucket into 1–6.

**What to look for when you read them later:**
- If NPS is below 8 on average, you have a bigger problem than any single critique.
- If Question 3 ("where did you check out") keeps naming the same beat, that beat is broken. The persona test predicted Block 2 (the framework/bad-prompt contrast) would be that beat. Check whether the real feedback agrees.
- If Question 6 answers are mostly "maybe" with vague reasons, your value proposition is unclear. A "maybe" that says "because I need to see if my team would benefit" is a champion signal. A "maybe" that says "because I'm not sure it's worth it" is a positioning problem.
- If Question 7 is mostly blank, people didn't feel safe enough to say the hard thing. Anonymous didn't work. Something about the in-person handout or the group dynamic killed the privacy.
- If Question 5 is mostly "probably not," the lesson produced emotion but no behavior change. That's a failure mode worth taking seriously even if NPS is high.

**After the run, save each survey result (or Google Form export) in this folder alongside the run file.** The raw data becomes part of the historical record for this cohort.
