# Article 1 Rewrite: Before and After

**Article:** "Your AI Training Budget Is a Line Item, Not a Strategy"
**Date:** 2026-04-03
**Framework applied:** McKinsey-style editorial standards (see `2026-04-03-mckinsey-style-editorial.md`)

---

## Title

**Before:** Your AI Training Budget Is a Line Item, Not a Strategy
**After:** AI Training Budgets Are Growing. Workforce Capability Isn't.

**Why:** The original addresses "you" directly (sales register) and uses a clever wordplay ("line item, not a strategy") that feels like a LinkedIn post. The rewrite states a finding — a gap between two trends — that the article will prove. It's a declarative claim, not a provocation.

---

## Subtitle

**Before:** Companies are spending more on AI training than ever. Most of it isn't changing behavior.
**After:** 86 percent of companies are increasing AI training budgets. Only 35 percent see results. That gap is where most of the money goes.

**Why:** The original is assertion-only. The rewrite leads with the stat, delivers the punchline fast, and lands on a line someone can repeat in a meeting. Short enough to paste into Slack. An HR Director doesn't need "structural problem in how organizations approach capability building" — they need "86% spend more, only 35% see results."

---

## Key Takeaways

**Before:**
1. 86% of companies are increasing AI budgets, but only 35% have a mature upskilling strategy
2. The three most common AI training investments — platform licenses, lunch-and-learns, and prompt workshops — share a common failure mode: none of them measure behavior change
3. The skill gap looks identical whether you're teaching university students or enterprise teams — it's a workflow problem, not a knowledge problem
4. Training becomes a strategy when it starts with specific pain points, embeds AI into existing workflows, and measures what people do differently

**After:**
1. 86% of companies are increasing AI budgets. Only 35% have a mature upskilling strategy. That gap compounds every quarter.
2. The three most popular investments — platform licenses, lunch-and-learns, and prompt workshops — all share one problem: none of them measure whether anyone works differently after.
3. The same skill gap shows up in a University of Chicago classroom and a Fortune 500 conference room. It's not a knowledge problem. It's a workflow problem.
4. Training becomes a strategy when it starts with a specific pain point, not "AI awareness" — and when someone measures what changes.

**Why:** Each takeaway now (a) leads with data or a specific observation, (b) fits in a Slack message, (c) can be repeated in a meeting without notes. Shorter sentences. One idea at a time. No word over two syllables when a simpler one works.

## Section 1: "The three things companies buy that don't work"

### Heading

**Before:** The three things companies buy that don't work
**After:** Three common investments, one shared failure mode

**Why:** The original sounds like a blog listicle. The rewrite is a declarative claim that frames what the section will prove.

### Opening Paragraph

**Before:**
> Most enterprise AI training budgets fund three things: platform licenses, lunch-and-learns, and prompt engineering workshops. That gap between 86% spending more and 35% doing it well? That's where the money disappears.

**After:**
> Most companies spend AI training budgets on three things: platform licenses (LinkedIn Learning, Coursera), lunch-and-learns, and prompt workshops. 86 percent of companies are spending more on AI training this year. Only 35 percent say it's working. That's a lot of money producing activity instead of results.

**Why:** Names specific products. Stat arrives fast with the punchline right after. "Activity instead of results" — a line an HR Director can borrow for their budget conversation. Three short sentences, not one 40-word analytical one.

### Platform Licenses Paragraphs

**Before:**
> Platform licenses are the default. Someone in L&D signs an enterprise agreement with LinkedIn Learning or Coursera, sends out an email, and waits. Completion rates hover between 5% and 15%. The content feels like 90s onboarding videos people tolerate rather than learn from.
>
> Six months later, someone pulls a usage report and finds 80% of employees haven't logged in since the first week. The license renews anyway. Canceling it would mean admitting the initiative failed.

**After:**
> Platform licenses are the default move. L&D signs a deal with LinkedIn Learning or Coursera, sends an email, and waits. Completion rates for self-paced AI courses: 5 to 15 percent. For comparison, instructor-led programs see above 85 percent.
>
> Six months in, 80 percent of employees haven't logged in since week one. The license renews anyway — canceling it would mean admitting the initiative failed.
an
**Why:** Added comparison anchor (instructor-led 85%+). Kept the "license renews anyway" observation — it's vivid and real. Cut "operating cost that produces no measurable capability change" — that's consultant-speak an HR Director would glaze past. The before version's "90s onboarding videos" was too casual; the first rewrite was too dense. This version hits the middle.

### Lunch-and-Learns Paragraph

**Before:**
> Lunch-and-learns attract people who want free food. They're taught by whoever has time to dip out for an hour, not by practitioners who've embedded AI into real work. I've talked to L&D directors who run these monthly and have never once followed up to see if a single attendee applied anything.

**After:**
> Lunch-and-learns have a different problem. They're led by whoever's available, not by someone who's built AI into real work. The sessions generate attendance, not application.
>
> I've talked to L&D directors who run these monthly. Not one has followed up to check whether anyone applied a single thing.

**Why:** Kept "I've talked to" — this is one of the 2-3 "I" moments per article, and it's powerful because it's specific. Short paragraphs. "Generate attendance, not application" stays — it's the kind of tight distinction the reader will borrow. Removed "free food" line (too flippant) and "production workflows" (too corporate).

### Prompt Workshops Paragraph

**Before:**
> Prompt engineering workshops are the newest flavor. Entire half-day sessions dedicated to crafting the perfect prompt. But prompting is a tactic, not the skill. Teaching someone to write better prompts without teaching them where AI fits in their work is like teaching someone to type faster without teaching them what to write.

**After:**
> Prompt workshops are the newest thing. Half-day sessions on crafting the perfect prompt. But prompting is to AI what typing is to writing — it's the input, not the skill.
>
> Teaching someone to prompt better without showing them where AI fits in their work is like teaching someone to type faster without teaching them what to write.

**Why:** Kept the typing analogy — it's the best line in the article and HR Directors will repeat it. Shortened everything around it. "The newest thing" is simpler than "newest flavor" (which tried too hard) and "newest category of spend" (which tried too hard in the other direction). Two short paragraphs instead of one dense one.

### Measurement Paragraph

**Before:**
> What ties all three together: nobody measures whether any of it worked. Most enterprise leaders have no framework for measuring AI training ROI. They rely on anecdotal evidence and sentiment surveys. Ask how training changed behavior and you'll hear "people seem more comfortable" or "engagement was positive." That's not measurement. That's hope.

**After:**
> What all three have in common: nobody measures whether anything changed. Ask how training went and you hear "people seem more comfortable" or "engagement was positive."
>
> That's sentiment. It's not measurement. Sentiment tells you how people feel about AI. It doesn't tell you whether anyone works differently.

**Why:** The original's "That's not measurement. That's hope." is a great mic drop — but it's blog voice. The first McKinsey rewrite ("sentiment indicators, not performance metrics") was too dense. This version keeps the punch but adds the one-sentence explanation that makes the distinction useful: "Sentiment tells you how people feel. It doesn't tell you whether anyone works differently." That's a line an HR Director can use in their next budget review.

## Section 2: "What a training strategy looks like"

### Heading

**Before:** What a training strategy looks like
**After:** From line item to capability investment: what changes structurally

**Why:** Declarative claim that previews the section's argument.

### Key Paragraph Changes

**Before:**
> A strategy starts with a specific workflow pain point, not "AI awareness." You identify the three to five tasks in a department that consume disproportionate time relative to their strategic value.

**After:**
> A strategy starts with a specific pain point, not "AI awareness." Which tasks eat the most time for the least value? The operations team compiling weekly status reports. The legal team reviewing vendor contracts. The finance team writing monthly commentary. Start there.

**Why:** Kept simple language. Added three concrete examples — name things, don't talk about categories. Each example gets its own beat. Short enough to scan.

**Before:**
> The best programs I've seen track a simple metric: can this person now do in thirty minutes what used to take them three hours?

**After:**
> The best programs track one metric: can someone now do in 30 minutes what used to take 3 hours? If yes, training worked. If they can't name a task that changed, it didn't.

**Why:** Simple pass/fail framing. No "depreciating asset" or "capability vs familiarity" — HR Directors skim past that. The yes/no test is something they can bring to their next check-in.

## Section 3: "The line-item test"

### Heading

**Before:** The line-item test
**After:** Where AI training sits in the budget tells you everything

**Why:** Shorter. Still a claim. "Tells you everything" is confident without being academic.

### Key Change

**Before:**
> Pull up your company's L&D budget. Where does AI training sit? If it's a line item next to compliance training...

**After:**
> Look at where AI training sits in your L&D budget. If it's next to compliance training and the annual leadership retreat, it will deliver exactly the same results: a checkbox.

**Why:** Kept the direct "you" — this section is making the reader do something, and that's the point. Shortened. "A checkbox" lands harder than "categories designed to check boxes, not change how work gets done."

## Section 4: "What we see from both sides"

### Heading

**Before:** What we see from both sides
**After:** The skill gap looks the same in a graduate classroom and a Fortune 500 conference room

**Why:** States the finding. "Looks the same" is simpler than "is identical across both populations."

### Opening

**Before:**
> I teach 75+ students at the University of Chicago and lead AI efforts at a $40B enterprise, so I see both sides. The skill gap is identical.

**After:**
> I teach 75+ students at the University of Chicago and lead AI at a $40B enterprise. The skill gap is the same on both sides.

**Why:** Keep the "I" — this is the proprietary observation section and it's one of 2-3 "I" uses in the whole article. Don't bury it in a gerund construction. State it plainly. "I see both sides" was slightly informal; "the skill gap is the same on both sides" says it without the aside.

### Closing

**Before:**
> Until training gets that concrete, it stays theoretical. And theoretical training is what a line item buys you.

**After:**
> Until training gets that specific, it stays theoretical. And theoretical training is what a line item buys.

**Why:** Almost unchanged. The original was already good. Swapped "concrete" for "specific" (simpler word). Dropped "you" at the end — the sentence is stronger without it. No need for a McKinsey "the question is not X but Y" construction here — that would be overwriting a line that already works.

## What Changed Structurally (Summary)

| Dimension | Before | After |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Reading level | College essay | HBR blog — scannable, quotable |
| Paragraph length | 4-6 sentences | 2-3 sentences max |
| Data handling | Stats cited but unanchored | Every stat has a comparison ("5-15% vs 85%+") |
| Paragraph structure | Point, point, point, point | Claim, evidence, so-what |
| Headings | Clever/catchy | Claims the section will prove |
| Section endings | Summary or mic-drop | Line the reader can borrow |
| Vocabulary | Blog ("newest flavor") OR dense consulting ("capability investment") | Middle: clear, direct, no jargon but not casual |
| "I" usage | Scattered | 2-3x total, saved for proprietary observations |
| Forwarding test | Reader nods along | Reader copy-pastes a line into Slack |
| "While...only" constructions | 0 | 2 |
| Comparison anchors | 0 | 3 |
